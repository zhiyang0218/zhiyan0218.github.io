Projects & Experiences

Robotic De-Powdering System for Additive Manufacturing

In automotive manufacturing, many “green parts” require extremely high dimensional accuracy but are fragile and easily damaged. These parts are often produced using metal laser-sintering additive manufacturing. However, after printing, workers must manually excavate them from powder beds—a slow, labor-intensive process that exposes workers to metallic dust and risks part breakage. This project aims to develop a fully automated robotic de-powdering system that can safely locate, excavate, and grasp delicate 3D-printed parts without human involvement.

(Concept Photo)

Methods & System Design

The system consists of two coordinated robotic arms:

  • Primary Manipulator: A DENSO robotic arm controlled through ROS1, equipped with a RealSense 515 LiDAR for depth sensing. This arm performs precision grasping and part placement.
  • Excavation Arm: A second robotic arm equipped with a vacuum tool that levels the powder bed and exposes deeper layers of parts.

After each layer of parts is removed, the LiDAR captures a 3D point cloud of the uneven powder surface. A custom vacuum-based path planning algorithm computes the trajectory needed to flatten the surface, enabling consistent multi-layer excavation.

I designed and improved a modular gripping mechanism in Fusion 360 to protect fragile parts during grasping while supporting quick configuration changes.

Results

The prototype system successfully automated the de-powdering process for multi-layer powder beds. Key achievements include:

  • Accurate part localization through LiDAR depth sensing
  • Reliable, damage-free grasping of fragile components
  • Layer-by-layer excavation with automated powder smoothing
  • Reduced manual labor and minimized worker exposure to metallic dust

Evaluation

The system demonstrated strong effectiveness in improving safety and consistency. While extremely fine powder adhesion remains a challenge, the modular gripper and sensing workflow showed strong potential for industrial adoption. Future improvements include higher-resolution sensing and learning-based excavation strategies.
