Robotic De-Powdering System for Additive Manufacturing

In automotive manufacturing, many “green parts” require extremely high dimensional accuracy but are fragile and easily damaged. These parts are often produced using metal laser-sintering additive manufacturing. However, after printing, workers must manually excavate them from powder beds—a slow, labor-intensive process that exposes workers to metallic dust and risks part breakage. This project aims to develop a fully automated robotic de-powdering system that can safely locate, excavate, and grasp delicate 3D-printed parts without human involvement.

(Concept Photo)

Methods & System Design

The system consists of two coordinated robotic arms:

  • Primary Manipulator: A DENSO robotic arm controlled through ROS1, equipped with a RealSense 515 LiDAR for depth sensing. This arm performs precision grasping and part placement.
  • Excavation Arm: A second robotic arm equipped with a vacuum tool that levels the powder bed and exposes deeper layers of parts.

After each layer of parts is removed, the LiDAR captures a 3D point cloud of the uneven powder surface. A custom vacuum-based path planning algorithm computes the trajectory needed to flatten the surface, enabling consistent multi-layer excavation.

I designed and improved a modular gripping mechanism in Fusion 360 to protect fragile parts during grasping while supporting quick configuration changes.

Results

The prototype system successfully automated the de-powdering process for multi-layer powder beds. Key achievements include:

  • Accurate part localization through LiDAR depth sensing
  • Reliable, damage-free grasping of fragile components
  • Layer-by-layer excavation with automated powder smoothing
  • Reduced manual labor and minimized worker exposure to metallic dust

Evaluation

The system demonstrated strong effectiveness in improving safety and consistency. While extremely fine powder adhesion remains a challenge, the modular gripper and sensing workflow showed strong potential for industrial adoption. Future improvements include higher-resolution sensing and learning-based excavation strategies.

Wheel-Legged Urban Inspection Robot – FDIsystems

As cities continue to expand, there is a growing need for reliable, efficient, and safe inspection of urban infrastructure such as industrial plants, utility corridors, and building interiors. Traditional inspection tasks often require human workers to enter narrow, complex, or hazardous environments. FDIsystems launched a new robotics division to address this challenge by developing a wheel-legged inspection robot that combines the efficiency of two-wheeled balancing with enhanced terrain adaptability. The goal of this project was to create a robot capable of navigating typical urban environments, including stairs, small gaps, and uneven terrain, while keeping development cost and system complexity manageable.

(Prototype of the wheel-legged inspection robot)

Structure Design & Internal Layout

The company chose a wheel-legged configuration to balance cost, maturity of control algorithms, and terrain capability. Starting from a two-wheeled self-balancing base, the robot is equipped with two additional legs to enable stair-climbing and small obstacle traversal. During my internship, I designed the mechanical structure of the robot and optimized the internal layout of key components.

I focused on the arrangement of batteries, controllers, motors, and sensors to improve space utilization and maintain a low center of mass for stability. Through multiple design iterations, I achieved roughly a 20% increase in internal space utilization, making it easier to integrate future modules such as sensors or communication hardware.

Assembly, Testing & Troubleshooting

After the design phase, I led the assembly of the prototype robots and organized structural-functional tests. These tests included:

  • Balancing performance and motion stability under different loads
  • Interaction between wheel drive and leg mechanisms
  • Performance of new motors in serial robot configurations

During testing, we encountered stability issues in robots equipped with newly sourced motors. I traced the problem to incorrect motor specifications provided by the vendor and worked with the team to verify torque, speed, and power requirements. This prevented further integration issues and unnecessary hardware damage.

Results

By the end of the internship, the team had a functional prototype with:

  • Improved internal space utilization, enabling cleaner wiring and easier maintenance
  • Verified structural robustness under repeated motion tests
  • Resolved motor-related stability issues through systematic troubleshooting

The prototype established a solid foundation for future development of urban inspection capabilities such as stair climbing and small obstacle traversal.

Evaluation & Impact

This project demonstrated that a wheel-legged architecture can be a practical compromise between cost, control complexity, and terrain adaptability. While advanced locomotion such as full stair-climbing still requires further development, the structural design and component layout greatly improved maintainability and upgradability. The work also highlighted the importance of carefully validating vendor specifications for critical components like motors.

My Contribution

As an intern at FDIsystems in Anhui, China, I:

  • Designed the structure of the wheel-legged robot and optimized the internal layout, improving space utilization by about 20%
  • Led assembly, structural-functional testing, and troubleshooting of the robot prototypes
  • Identified incorrect motor specifications supplied by the vendor and helped resolve stability issues in serial robots with new motors
  • Coordinated the procurement and customization of key components, including motors and batteries

BRalgas Seaweed Fixed Bed & Harvesting System

BRalgas operates seaweed farms in tropical waters off the coast of Brazil, cultivating seaweed for applications in the food and pharmaceutical industries. Although the economic potential is high, the harvesting process was largely manual: workers had to jump into the water, pull seaweed up by hand or with nets, load it into bags, and lift the bags back onto the boat. This workflow was slow, physically demanding, and exposed workers to a high risk of injury. Our project aimed to design a fixed-bed cultivation cage and mechanical harvesting system to improve worker safety and at least double harvesting efficiency.

(Concept rendering of the seaweed fixed bed and harvesting device)

System Design & Client Collaboration

As a core member of the team based in Madison, WI, I helped bridge communication between the client in Brazil and our design team. I initiated and chaired weekly meetings with the client to clarify user requirements, review design updates, and prioritize new features. Every two weeks, we delivered updated designs for both the seaweed cultivation cage and the crane boom to keep pace with evolving operational needs and constraints in the open-sea environment.

Crane Boom & Structural Analysis

I modeled different crane boom configurations in SolidWorks, taking into account:

  • Load from wet seaweed and water-filled nets
  • Boat deck mounting constraints
  • Operator reach, safety margins, and stability

Using finite element analysis (FEA), I evaluated stress, deflection, and safety factors under various loading scenarios. These simulations helped us identify suitable cross-sections and materials while ensuring the boom remained safe and robust under real sea conditions.

Harvesting Device Design

To replace manual lifting, I designed and helped fabricate a mechanical seaweed harvesting device that could be deployed from the crane boom. The device was engineered to:

  • Capture and lift seaweed from the cultivation cage with minimal manual handling
  • Interface cleanly with the fixed-bed structure
  • Be simple to maintain in a marine environment

Early tests and time estimates suggested that the new device could approximately double worker harvesting efficiency compared to the original fully manual process.

(Prototype or animation of the seaweed harvesting process)

Results

By the end of the project period (Oct. 2023 – May 2024), we delivered:

  • Refined fixed-bed cage and crane boom designs that matched updated client requirements
  • FEA-validated crane boom structures with appropriate safety factors
  • A working harvesting device concept and fabricated prototype aimed at doubling worker efficiency

Evaluation & Impact

The project demonstrated that a fixed-bed and crane-assisted harvesting system can significantly reduce the physical burden on workers and lower injury risk while increasing throughput. Ongoing work focuses on field testing in real sea conditions and refining the device for long-term durability and ease of use in tropical marine environments.

  • Initiated and chaired weekly meetings between team members and the client
  • Updated seaweed cultivation cage and crane boom designs every two weeks to reflect evolving user needs
  • Modeled crane boom structures and performed FEA in SolidWorks
  • Designed and helped fabricate seaweed harvesting devices that aimed to double worker efficiency
